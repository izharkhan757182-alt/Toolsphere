How to Use a QR Code Generator (Complete Guide)

ToolsPhere Team · Guide · 6 min read

QR codes have quietly become one of the simplest bridges between the physical and digital world. Point a phone camera at a small square of black-and-white pattern, and it can open a website, save a contact, or connect to Wi-Fi in under a second. This guide walks through how to actually make one that works well, not just one that looks like it should.

What a QR code can hold

Most people assume QR codes only hold links, but they can carry any short block of text. That includes plain URLs, phone numbers formatted for dialing, email addresses, and even Wi-Fi network credentials in a specific text format that phones recognize automatically.

Choosing colors that still scan

A QR code is read by contrast, not by color itself. Scanners look for a clear difference between the "dark" and "light" modules of the pattern. If you customize colors in a generator, keep the foreground noticeably darker than the background — pastel-on-white or dark-on-dark combinations are the most common reason a "working" code fails to scan on someone else's phone.

Picking the right size

Size matters more for print than for screens. As a rough rule, a code meant to be scanned from arm's length (like a poster or table tent) should be printed at least 2.5–3 cm (about an inch) wide. Codes displayed at a distance — on a billboard or presentation slide — need to scale up proportionally with viewing distance.

Always test before you publish

It sounds obvious, but the single most common QR mistake is publishing a code that was never actually tested. Before printing flyers, business cards, or packaging, scan the generated code with two or three different phones to confirm it opens the right destination.

Quick tip: Test your printed QR code at the actual size it will appear in real life — a code that scans fine on your laptop screen can behave differently once shrunk down to fit a business card.

Static vs. dynamic QR codes

The QR codes created by most free generators, including this one, are "static" — the destination is baked directly into the pattern and can't be changed later. That's fine for most uses. If you need to update the destination after printing (common for large print runs), you'd need a "dynamic" QR service that redirects through a short link, which is a different kind of tool entirely.

A QR code is only as useful as the moment someone actually scans it — design for the phone camera, not just the screen you're looking at right now.

Common reasons a QR code fails to scan

  • Low contrast between foreground and background colors
  • Code printed too small relative to the scanning distance
  • Damage or creases across the pattern (common on folded flyers)
  • Too much surrounding clutter competing for the camera's focus
