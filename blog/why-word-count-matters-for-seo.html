Why Word Count Still Matters for SEO in 2026

ToolsPhere Team · SEO · 5 min read

"How many words should my article be?" is one of the most common SEO questions, and also one of the most misunderstood. There's no magic number that guarantees a top ranking — but length still correlates with something search engines genuinely care about: whether a page actually answers the question someone typed in.

Length is a symptom, not a cause

Longer pages tend to rank well not because they're long, but because covering a topic thoroughly usually requires more words. A 2,000-word guide that answers five related questions a reader might have will often outperform a 300-word page that only half-answers one. The word count is a side effect of depth, not the goal itself.

Where a counter actually helps

Word and character counters are most useful for the constraints that are hard rules, not soft guidelines: meta descriptions that get cut off past a certain character count, YouTube descriptions with visible-text limits, or social captions with strict character caps. That's where checking your count before publishing saves you from content getting awkwardly truncated.

Keyword density: use it as a sanity check, not a target

Older SEO advice pushed people to hit a specific keyword density percentage. Modern search engines are far better at understanding meaning and context, so stuffing a phrase repeatedly can actually hurt readability and rankings. A keyword density check is best used the other way around — as a quick sanity check that you haven't accidentally repeated one word so often it reads unnaturally.

Reading time sets reader expectations

Showing an estimated reading time isn't an SEO ranking factor directly, but it affects whether someone commits to reading — and pages people actually read tend to perform better on the engagement signals search engines do track, like time on page.

Write for the person who typed the search query first. The word count that satisfies them is usually the same word count that satisfies the algorithm.
Quick tip: Before publishing, run your draft past a keyword density check as a sanity test — if one word appears far more than any other, it's often a sign you've repeated yourself rather than added depth.

A simple pre-publish checklist

  • Does the piece fully answer the question the title promises?
  • Is any section padded just to hit a target length?
  • Would a reader skimming the first two lines know whether to keep reading?
  • Does the meta description fit within its character limit without being cut off?
