7 Resume Mistakes That Get You Rejected

ToolsPhere Team · Career · 6 min read

Most resumes aren't rejected because the candidate lacks the skills — they're rejected because of avoidable formatting and content issues that make a hiring manager (or an applicant tracking system) move on in seconds.

1. Burying your most relevant experience

Recruiters often spend under 10 seconds on a first pass. If your most relevant experience is in the middle of a long list, it may never get seen. Put your strongest, most relevant role first — not necessarily your most recent one if it's less relevant.

2. Describing duties instead of results

"Responsible for managing social media" tells a hiring manager what you were assigned. "Grew Instagram following from 2,000 to 15,000 in eight months" tells them what you achieved. Wherever possible, swap duty-based lines for outcome-based ones.

Quick tip: For every bullet point, ask "so what?" — if the line doesn't imply a result or impact, it's a duty description, not an achievement.

3. Inconsistent formatting

Mismatched fonts, inconsistent date formats, and uneven spacing signal carelessness before a single word of content is read. Pick one format for dates, one font pairing, and stick to it throughout.

4. Fighting the applicant tracking system

Many companies scan resumes with software before a human sees them. Tables, text boxes, and unusual fonts can confuse this software and drop your information entirely. A clean, single-column layout with standard section headers (Experience, Education, Skills) parses far more reliably.

The best-formatted resume is the one that's boring to look at and easy to scan — save the personality for the interview.

5. A generic, one-size-fits-all summary

"Hardworking professional seeking new opportunities" says nothing a hiring manager can act on. A summary tailored to the specific role — mentioning the actual skills and experience that match the job posting — does far more work in three lines.

6. Listing skills without evidence

Anyone can list "leadership" as a skill. Pairing it with a specific example in your experience section (e.g. "led a team of 5 through a product launch") is what actually convinces a reader it's true.

7. Typos and inconsistent tense

Switching between past and present tense within the same section, or a typo in the first line, undermines an otherwise strong resume. Read it aloud before sending — it catches errors your eyes skim past silently.
